What has changed

  • This is no longer a small set of isolated projects.
  • The portfolio already includes visible outputs, faculty training, and named approval requests.
  • A check-in survey sent to researchers and project teams shows the same pattern beyond this immediate portfolio.

Institutional implication

  • The issue is no longer just access to tools or models.

  • NYU can either define that support model explicitly or continue to absorb it informally through individual projects.
Architecture Choices

Peers are making different AI architecture choices

Across peers, the difference is where each institution builds versus buys: interface, sandbox, enterprise stack, or gateway. NYU uses Portkey at the gateway layer.

In-house interface layer

CHAT at Columbia and Clarity Platform at Yale are institution-defined front doors.

Institution-operated research layer

AI Sandbox at Princeton and AI Gateway at Cornell support secure experimentation and development.

Vendor-led stack or gateway

Copilot Chat, Gemini, and NotebookLM at Rutgers; Portkey at NYU.

Products and architecture choices

Vendor-tool strategy centered on licensed enterprise products rather than a university-built platform layer.
NYU
Portkey LLM Gateway
Vendor-gateway strategy: Portkey sits at the control layer for routing, guardrails, usage visibility, budget controls, and application development.

What the architecture choices show

  • Some peers are building their own user-facing AI layer.
  • Some are operating secure research environments on top of vendor-managed model services.
  • Some are standardizing on licensed enterprise tools instead of building a local platform.
  • NYU's current model is different: a vendor gateway inside institutional infrastructure.
Across the region, the direction is similar: governed access for general users, plus a second layer for research and application development. The difference is whether that layer is built in house, assembled from vendors, or both.
Recommended Next Steps

What leadership can do next.

The portfolio, researcher feedback, and peer-university patterns point to a small set of practical moves that would help NYU stay current, increase usage, and keep researchers working through institutional pathways.

Invest in customer success, not only platform operations Add ongoing office hours, workshops, consultations, and lightweight follow-up so researchers have support after first access, similar to Harvard's Generative AI Support model and Stanford's AI Catalyst consultation layer.
Keep the institutional stack current Treat model availability, vendor changes, routing, and workflow updates as a standing review function. PI feedback suggests that even a short lag can affect active research work.
Separate the offering into clear layers Distinguish broad-access tools, research and developer workflows, and higher-touch implementation support so researchers know where to start and when to escalate, following patterns visible in Harvard's AI Sandbox and Stanford's AI Playground.
Add a secure pathway for higher-risk data A low/medium-risk-only model will push serious research teams off the institutional path. Yale's Clarity Platform supports low, medium, and most high-risk data except ePHI, Harvard's AI Sandbox supports up to Level 3 confidential data, and Princeton routes restricted work into Citadel.
Use vendors strategically, but keep institutional control points Peer schools are blending institutional layers with vendor and open-source components rather than building everything from scratch. Columbia's CHAT is built on LibreChat and paired with vendor tools, Princeton's AI Sandbox is an institution-operated secure environment for model experimentation, and Yale's Clarity Platform provides access to models from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google.
Add a grants and proposal lane Support opportunity discovery, technical framing, prototypes, and proposal development earlier so promising research ideas connect more directly to funding.
